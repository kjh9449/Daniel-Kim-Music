Hello, my name is Daniel Kim and I am a piano teacher

People usually assume that music can only be learned at a young age in order for a person to become eventually proficient, and for good reasons. It is true that many musicians have begun playing their instruments as young as 3. But perhaps it is for that same reason that mature students face a greater challenge in finding the right resources, support group, and teachers. I started learning the piano at the age of 16, at which point I only knew how to read a single note (out of the 88 notes in the piano) - middle G. I knew I wanted to play the first movement of Beethoven’s “Moonlight” sonata, but at the time it felt like an impossible task for me to accomplish. After all, I could only read one note and I would have to figure out all the other notes by determining how far apart they were from that familiar friend of mine, middle G. Never mind all the odd signs that look like “#” or letter “b” or even an occasional “x,” the whole left hand had a different system of music note coordination altogether from that of the right hand. And yet my drive to play the music was so powerful that I could not resist taking the first step, and the second, and the third. Note by note I deciphered what note was to be played and its subsequent rhythm. Without a teacher or anyone to ask for help (and at the time, YouTube either did not exist or it was not big enough for me even think to consult it), it was a fierce struggle one note after another. More than 10 years have passed since then and I am now a firm believer that mature beginner students, whom I define as age 14 and up, can indeed become excellent performers. Mature beginner students may face a unique set of challenges, different from that of younger students. Their bodies tend to be less flexible and more prone to tension. Because of that, the student often begins to develop a potentially harmful habit without knowing it, and proper posture must be taught from the very beginning in order to prevent any future injuries. Furthermore, being a mature student usually means the need to balance the busy-ness of life with the want to learn music. In fact, spending just 30 minutes everyday practicing may feel almost impossible for some. The great news is: learning music is not about spending a certain amount of time, but about the consistent musical problem solving. And mature students can utilize their strengths to be hyper-productive and efficient with how much ever their time-budget allows. They can learn to strategize when complex musical problems arise, and they can use their well developed will power to focus, without the need for any candy bribery from the teacher. Thus, mature students will benefit from an instructor who utilizes their important strengths and give appropriate guidance for their potential weaknesses. Music education does not need to be daunting for mature students. I firmly believe that every students can surprise themselves to learn what can be accomplished with the right support and the right instruction at every age. I understand the challenges that a mature student can go through because I have been there. I tailor my teaching so that I give the support and instruction I wish I received as a mature beginner student. Regardless of your age, I believe that you too can become an excellent performer.
